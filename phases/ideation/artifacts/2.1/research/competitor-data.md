## SAM Estimation

### Method 1: Top-Down from TAM

**Calculation**:
```
SAM = TAM × Geographic % × Segment % × Use-Case %
```

**Filters**:
- Geographic: 60% (Karachi represents ~60% of Pakistan urban grocery delivery market due to population density, digital payment penetration)
- Segment: 40% (Target ICP: working professionals, university students, housewives, small families — excludes rural, ultra-budget, and cash-only segments)
- Use-Case: 80% (Grocery delivery applicable to 80% of urban households, excluding those with no delivery access or preferring traditional shopping)

**Assumptions**:
- Pakistan online grocery/food delivery TAM: Estimated $500-800M (2024)
  - Foodpanda Pandamart estimated $150-200M Pakistan
  - Daraz Group $127M (grocery is <20% = ~$25M Pakistan grocery)
  - Krave Mart $5-10M
  - Others $50-100M combined
  - Total visible market: ~$250-350M (direct competitors)
  - Adding informal/cash-based transactions: $500-800M total TAM

**SAM Estimate**:
```
SAM = $650M (midpoint TAM) × 60% (Karachi) × 40% (ICP segment) × 80% (use-case fit)
SAM = $650M × 0.192 = $125M
```

**SAM Range**: $96M - $154M (using $500M and $800M TAM endpoints)

### Method 2: Bottom-Up from Competitors

**Calculation**:
```
SAM = Σ(Competitor Revenues in Karachi segment)
```

**Competitor Revenues in Karachi** (estimates):
- Foodpanda Pandamart (Karachi): $80-100M (50-60% of Pakistan revenue, Karachi is largest market)
- Krave Mart (Karachi): $5-10M (Karachi HQ, primary market)
- DealCart (Karachi): $1-3M (Karachi focus)
- GrocerApp (Karachi): $2-5M (Lahore primary, Karachi secondary)
- Daraz Grocery (Karachi): $10-15M (estimated 60% of Pakistan grocery in Karachi)
- Careem Now (Karachi): $5-10M (estimated Karachi grocery)
- Others: $5-10M

**Total SAM** (Bottom-Up): $108M - $153M

**Cross-Check**:
- Method 1 (Top-down): $96M - $154M
- Method 2 (Bottom-up): $108M - $153M
- **Convergence**: $108M - $153M (high confidence range)
- **Best Estimate SAM**: $130M (Karachi grocery delivery market)

## Market Context: Lessons from Airlift Shutdown (Case Study)

### Airlift — Pakistan's Cautionary Tale

**Company Profile**:
- **Founded**: 2019 (mass transit), pivoted to quick commerce 2020
- **Funding**: $85M total raised (Pakistan's largest startup funding at the time)
- **Valuation**: $275M peak valuation
- **Shutdown**: July 2022 (after 3 years)

**Why Airlift Failed** (lessons for market):
1. **Unsustainable Unit Economics**: Prioritized growth over profitability, cash burn exceeded revenue
2. **Global VC Winter**: Raised capital during 2020-2021 boom, unable to secure follow-on funding in 2022 downturn
3. **Pivot Risk**: Shifted from mass transit (working model) to quick commerce (unproven in Pakistan) without validating unit economics
4. **High CAC**: Dark store model + customer acquisition in emerging market = expensive, long payback periods
5. **Competition**: Foodpanda (Delivery Hero backing) and emerging players undercut pricing

**Market Impact**:
- Consolidation: Foodpanda and Krave Mart gained market share post-Airlift
- Investor Caution: Pakistan startup funding dropped from $150M (2022 peak) to more conservative levels
- Unit Economics Focus: Survivors (Krave Mart) emphasize path to profitability vs. pure growth
- Validation: Market exists (demand proven), but requires capital efficiency and sustainable model

**Implications for Karachi Grocery App**:
- Must demonstrate unit economics and path to profitability early
- Avoid pure growth-at-all-costs strategy (Airlift's mistake)
- Asset-light kiryana store partnership model > capital-intensive dark stores
- Traffic-aware routing and real-time inventory = differentiation + operational efficiency
- Pakistan market requires localization, not copy-paste of international models
