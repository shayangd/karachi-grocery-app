## Overview

### Venture Context Summary

**Problem**: Karachi citizens struggle with inconsistent grocery availability, rising prices, traffic congestion, and unpredictable store timings, making basic grocery shopping exhausting and time-consuming. This affects working families, students, and professionals who need to shop frequently (3-5 times per week) but face traffic bottlenecks, delivery delays, and out-of-stock frustrations.

**Solution**: A hyperlocal, real-time grocery availability app that shows live inventory from neighborhood kiryana stores, minimarts, and vendors with traffic-aware delivery routing for 30-45 minute delivery. Unlike competitors (Daraz Mart, PandaMart), the app provides real-time neighborhood store inventory visibility AND dynamic delivery estimates based on actual Karachi traffic conditions.

**Current Alternatives**: Daraz Mart, PandaMart, physical kiryana stores, large supermarkets (Imtiaz, Carrefour)

### Interview Goals and Success Criteria

**Primary Goals**:
1. Validate problem severity and frequency (Pain Score ‚â•7/10 for 70%+ interviews)
2. Confirm recent, recurring problem occurrence (within last 7 days for 80%+ interviews)
3. Identify existing workarounds and current spend
4. Assess solution resonance and willingness to switch
5. Establish willingness-to-pay range for pricing strategy

**Success Criteria (Recipe 1.1 KPIs)**:
- **I1.1 Pain Score**: ‚â•7/10 for 70%+ interviews (strong problem)
- **I1.2 Recency**: 80%+ experienced problem in last 7 days
- **I1.3 Workarounds**: 60%+ have active workarounds (validates severity)
- **I1.4 Solution Interest**: 70%+ show genuine interest
- **I1.5 Displacement**: 60%+ would replace current solution
- **I1.6 Outcome Alignment**: 70%+ align with desired outcomes
- **I1.7 Budget Availability**: 60%+ have allocated budget
- **I1.8 Current Spend**: Median monthly spend ‚â• $30
- **I1.9 WTP Analysis**: Van Westendorp pricing sensitivity data
- **I1.10 Purchase Intent**: 60%+ score 4-5/5 (likely to purchase)

## Target Personas

- **Segment**: Mid-Income Urban Professionals
- **Role**: Office worker, corporate employee, banker, consultant
- **Demographics**:
  - Age: 25-40 years
  - Income: PKR 80,000-250,000/month
  - Location: DHA, Clifton, Gulshan, PECHS, Nazimabad
  - Family: Often married with 1-2 children or young nuclear family
- **Shopping Behavior**:
  - Frequency: 3-5 times per week
  - Time constraint: Limited evening/weekend time
  - Pain points: Traffic after work, closed stores, delivery delays
- **Current Solutions**:
  - PandaMart, Daraz Mart for quick items
  - Weekend trips to Imtiaz/Carrefour
  - Emergency trips to local kiryana stores
- **Why Interview**: Primary target customer with highest willingness to pay, experiences all core pain points (traffic, time, inconsistent availability), has disposable income for convenience
- **Interview Focus**:
  - Problem severity during weekday evenings
  - Traffic impact on shopping decisions
  - Current delivery app frustrations
  - Willingness to pay for reliability
- **Expected Distribution**: 5-6 interviews (40-50% of total)

### Persona 2: Homemaker/Stay-at-Home Parent (Primary User)
- **Segment**: Household Managers
- **Role**: Manages household grocery needs, meal planning, family shopping

  - Location: Middle-class areas (Gulistan-e-Johar, North Nazimabad, FB Area, Malir Cantt)
  - Family: Typically 3-6 family members

  - Frequency: 4-6 times per week (fresh items)

- **Current Solutions**:
  - Daily kiryana store visits

- **Why Interview**: High-frequency user, experiences inventory inconsistency pain acutely, influences household spending decisions, sensitive to price and reliability

  - Fresh item availability frustrations

  - Price sensitivity thresholds
  - Social/sharing features interest

- **Role**: Kiryana store owner, minimart operator, small retailer

## Interview Script (27 Questions)

### Pre-Interview Setup

**Before the call:**
1. Confirm persona alignment (role, location, shopping habits)
2. Set expectations: 30-40 minutes, casual conversation
3. Get permission to take notes (no recording needed initially)
4. Have tracking template open

**Opening (2 minutes):**

This is pure research - I'm not selling anything today. I just want to understand your real experiences. There are no right or wrong answers; I'm just genuinely curious about your day-to-day reality.

Sound good? Great, let's dive in..."

## SECTION 1: PROBLEM DISCOVERY (20 minutes)
**Focus**: Past behavior, specific examples, pain intensity

---

### Q1: Tell me about the last time you went grocery shopping. Walk me through what happened.

**Purpose**: Establish recency, get specific details, understand current behavior (KPI I1.2)

**Probing Questions**:
- When exactly was this? (Day of week, time of day)
- What were you trying to buy?
- How long did the entire process take?
- Did anything go wrong or frustrate you?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Can't remember recent shopping trip (low frequency/low engagement)
- ‚ùå "It was fine, no issues" (no pain experienced)
- ‚ùå Very infrequent shopping (once a month or less)

**Scoring Notes**:
- Recency: Last 7 days = Recent, Last 30 days = Moderate, 30+ days = Low
- Note any specific frustrations mentioned

---

### Q2: How often do you typically need to buy groceries or household essentials?

**Purpose**: Confirm frequency, validate ICP assumption of 3-5x per week (KPI I1.2)

**Probing Questions**:
- Is this more or less frequent than you'd like? Why?
- What drives the frequency? (fresh items, storage, last-minute needs)
- Has this changed over time?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Once a month or less (not frequent enough to feel pain acutely)
- ‚ùå "Whenever I feel like it" (unpredictable, low commitment)

**Scoring Notes**:
- High frequency = 4-7x/week
- Medium = 2-3x/week
- Low = 1x/week or less

---

### Q3: Think back to the last month. Tell me about a time when grocery shopping was particularly frustrating or difficult. What happened?

**Purpose**: Identify pain intensity through specific story (KPI I1.1)

**Probing Questions**:
- What made that situation so frustrating?
- How did you feel at that moment?
- What did you end up doing?
- How much time did you lose?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Can't think of a frustrating experience
- ‚ùå Downplays frustration ("It wasn't that bad")
- ‚ùå Very rare occurrence ("This only happened once")

**Scoring Notes**:
- Listen for emotional language: "exhausted", "angry", "stressed"
- Note specific impact: time lost, meal plans ruined, family affected

---

### Q4: On a scale of 0-10, where 0 is "no problem at all" and 10 is "extremely painful and urgent to solve", how would you rate the frustration level of grocery shopping in Karachi?

**Purpose**: Quantify pain score directly (KPI I1.1)

**Probing Questions**:
- What makes it that specific number for you?
- What would have to change for it to be a 10? A 0?
- Has this gotten better or worse over the past year?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Score below 5 (mild problem)
- ‚ùå Can't articulate why they chose that number
- ‚ùå "It depends" without strong explanation

**Scoring Notes**:
- Record exact number
- Target: 7-10 for strong problem validation
- Note reasoning behind score

---

### Q5: What are the biggest challenges you face when buying groceries? Tell me about the last time each of these caused you a real problem.

**Purpose**: Identify pain hierarchy, validate assumed pain points (KPI I1.1)

**Probing Questions**:
- Which of these frustrates you the most?
- How often does [specific challenge] happen?
- When was the last time this happened? Walk me through it.

**Expected Pain Points to Listen For**:
- Traffic/travel time
- Store out of stock
- Unpredictable store hours
- Delivery delays/unreliability
- Price uncertainty
- Quality concerns
- Heavy bags/physical burden

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Only mentions price (not a convenience problem)
- ‚ùå Generic complaints without specific examples
- ‚ùå Different problems than expected (misaligned ICP)

**Scoring Notes**:
- Rank top 3 pain points
- Note which are most frequent and most severe

---

### Q6: What do you currently do to deal with these challenges? Walk me through your current approach.

**Purpose**: Identify existing workarounds (KPI I1.3), understand current solutions and their costs

**Probing Questions**:
- How long have you been doing this?
- How well does this work?
- What does this cost you (time, money, effort)?
- What happens when this workaround fails?

**Common Workarounds to Listen For**:
- Off-peak shopping times
- Bulk buying on weekends
- Multiple store visits
- Send family member
- Use delivery apps (PandaMart, Daraz)
- Keep extra inventory at home
- Adjust meal plans based on availability

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå "I don't do anything special" (low pain)
- ‚ùå Perfectly happy with current workaround (no need for alternative)

**Scoring Notes**:
- Active workaround = Pain is real
- Expensive/time-consuming workaround = Higher pain
- Multiple workarounds = Very high pain

---

### Q7: Tell me about a time when your current approach to grocery shopping completely failed. What happened and what did you do?

**Purpose**: Understand edge cases, failure modes, emotional impact

**Probing Questions**:
- How did this affect you or your family?
- What did you do to recover?
- How much did this cost you (time/money)?
- How often does this kind of failure happen?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Can't remember a failure (low stakes problem)
- ‚ùå "No big deal, I just dealt with it" (low emotional impact)

**Scoring Notes**:
- Note severity of failure impact
- Recovery cost (time, money, relationships)

---

### Q8: If you could wave a magic wand and fix ONE thing about grocery shopping in Karachi, what would it be and why?

**Purpose**: Understand top priority pain, validate solution direction

**Probing Questions**:
- Why that specific thing?
- How would your life be different if this was solved?
- What have you tried in the past to solve this yourself?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Wants something unrelated to our solution (e.g., "lower prices everywhere")
- ‚ùå Vague answer without passion
- ‚ùå Already solved by existing apps

**Scoring Notes**:
- Does their top priority align with our solution?
- Passion level when describing ideal solution

## SECTION 2: SOLUTION EXPLORATION (20 minutes)
**Focus**: Solution resonance, competitive displacement, outcome alignment

**Transition Statement:**

"Thanks for sharing all that - really helpful to understand what you're dealing with. Let me describe something I've been thinking about, and I'd love your honest reaction..."

**Solution Concept Presentation (Neutral, No Pitch):**

"Imagine an app where you can see real-time inventory from kiryana stores and minimarts in your neighborhood - so you know exactly what's available before ordering. The app would also show you delivery time estimates based on current traffic conditions in Karachi, so you'd get realistic delivery windows, not false promises. The goal is 30-45 minute delivery with actual reliability."

**Pause for initial reaction...**

### Q9: What's your initial reaction to this idea? What goes through your mind when you hear this?

**Purpose**: Capture authentic first impression (KPI I1.4)

**Probing Questions**:
- What specifically interests you? What concerns you?
- How is this different from what you use now?
- What questions come to mind?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Polite but unenthusiastic response
- ‚ùå "That's nice" without elaboration
- ‚ùå Immediate comparison dismissing the idea

**Scoring Notes**:
- Genuine excitement = Strong interest
- Thoughtful questions = Engaged interest
- Polite nodding = Weak interest

---

### Q10: Think about the last frustrating grocery shopping experience you described earlier. If this app existed, how would that situation have been different?

**Purpose**: Connect solution to real pain, test outcome alignment (KPI I1.6)

**Probing Questions**:
- Would this have actually solved your problem?
- What would you have done differently?
- How much time/hassle would this have saved?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå "Maybe it would help a little"
- ‚ùå Can't articulate concrete difference
- ‚ùå "I'm not sure it would have mattered"

**Scoring Notes**:
- Clear articulation of value = High alignment
- Vague benefits = Low alignment

---

### Q11: You mentioned you currently use [PandaMart/Daraz/kiryana stores/etc.]. How would this be different from what you're already doing?

**Purpose**: Assess differentiation perception and displacement potential (KPI I1.5)

**Probing Questions**:
- What does [current solution] not do that this would?
- Would you use this INSTEAD of [current solution] or IN ADDITION to?
- What would make you switch from [current solution] to this?

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå "Sounds the same as what I use"
- ‚ùå "I'd maybe try it once but stick with [current]"
- ‚ùå Can't see clear differentiation

**Scoring Notes**:
- Replacement intent = Strong displacement
- Addition to toolkit = Moderate displacement
- Unclear differentiation = Weak displacement

---

### Q12: What concerns or hesitations would you have about using something like this?

**Purpose**: Identify adoption barriers, surface hidden objections

**Probing Questions**:
- What would need to be true for you to overcome this concern?
- Have you had bad experiences with similar apps before?
- What would make you trust this enough to try?

**Common Concerns to Listen For**:
- Reliability/trust issues
- Data privacy
- Payment security
- Delivery quality
- Store partnership legitimacy
- App complexity

**Red Flags**:
- ‚ùå Long list of concerns with no resolution path
- ‚ùå Fundamental trust issues with category
- ‚ùå Concerns that are expensive to solve

**Scoring Notes**:
- Note severity: Dealbreaker vs manageable concern
- Willingness to overcome concerns

---

**Probing Questions**:

- Has this increased or decreased over the past year?

**Red Flags**:

### Q18: What do you currently pay for delivery when you use apps like PandaMart or Daraz? How do you feel about those fees?

- Current willingness to pay delivery fees
- Price sensitivity level
- Value perception of delivery

---

**Red Flags**:

- ‚ùå Can't articulate value exchange

**Scoring Notes**:

- Willingness for variable pricing

## The Mom Test Reminders

### Core Principles to Follow

1. **Talk About Their Life, Not Your Idea**
   - ‚ùå Bad: "Would you use an app that shows real-time inventory?"
   - ‚úÖ Good: "Tell me about the last time a store didn't have what you needed."

2. **Ask About Specifics in the Past, Not Hypotheticals**
   - ‚ùå Bad: "How often do you shop for groceries?"
   - ‚úÖ Good: "When was the last time you bought groceries? Walk me through it."

3. **Listen More Than You Talk**
   - Target: 70% them talking, 30% you asking
   - Use silence - let them fill it
   - Probing questions, not leading questions

4. **Don't Pitch, Don't Convince**
   - You're learning, not selling
   - Present solution concept neutrally
   - Listen for genuine resonance, not politeness

5. **Dig Into Emotions and Specifics**
   - "How did that make you feel?"
   - "What did you do next?"
   - "Why was that frustrating?"

6. **Watch for Compliments - They're Lies**
   - "This is a great idea!" = Politeness, not data
   - Redirect: "Thanks! Tell me about the last time you faced [problem]..."

7. **Anchor on Behavior, Not Opinions**
   - Opinions are cheap, behavior is real
   - Past actions predict future actions
   - Current spend indicates willingness to pay

8. **Embrace Bad News**
   - Negative feedback is more valuable than positive
   - Indifference is worse than hatred
   - If they're not excited, that's important data

### Phrase Substitutions

| ‚ùå Avoid | ‚úÖ Use Instead |
|---------|---------------|
| "Would you use this?" | "Tell me about the last time [problem occurred]" |
| "How much would you pay?" | "What do you currently spend on [solution]?" |
| "What features do you want?" | "What frustrates you about [current solution]?" |
| "Do you like this idea?" | "How do you currently handle [problem]?" |
| "Would you buy this?" | "Show me your receipt from [current solution]" |

### Red Flags to Watch For

üö© **Generic Praise**
- "This sounds really cool!"
- "I'd definitely use this"
- "Great idea, best of luck!"

üö© **Hypothetical Future Talk**
- "I would probably..."
- "I think I'd..."
- "In the future I might..."

üö© **Feature Requests Without Context**
- Long wish lists
- "You should add [feature X]"
- No mention of current pain or workarounds

üö© **Avoidance of Money Talk**
- Deflects pricing questions
- "It depends"
- Can't articulate current spend

### Good Signs to Look For

‚úÖ **Specific Recent Stories**
- Exact dates/times
- Emotional detail
- Concrete actions taken

‚úÖ **Active Workarounds**
- Currently spending time/money to solve
- Multiple attempted solutions
- Tolerance pain suggests high willingness to pay

‚úÖ **Unprompted Urgency**
- "When will this be available?"
- "Can I beta test?"
- "Let me know when it launches"

‚úÖ **Referral Offers**
- "You should talk to [friend]"
- "I know 5 people with this problem"
- Offers to introduce you

### Interview Anti-Patterns

‚ùå **The Pitch Trap**
- Spending more than 2 minutes describing solution
- Trying to convince them it's a good idea
- Defending against criticism

‚ùå **The Idea Validation Trap**
- Asking if they like your idea
- Seeking confirmation bias
- Ignoring negative signals

‚ùå **The Feature Trap**
- Getting lost in feature discussion
- Building their wish list instead of solving root problem
- Asking what features they want before confirming problem

‚ùå **The Compliment Trap**
- Taking "great idea!" as validation
- Not digging past politeness
- Ending interview on high note without hard data

### Recovery Phrases

When you catch yourself in a bad pattern:

**If you're pitching:**
"Actually, let me stop there - I'm more interested in understanding your situation. Tell me about..."

**If they're being too nice:**
"I appreciate that! But honestly, I need your critical feedback. What concerns you about this?"

**If it's too hypothetical:**
"Let's focus on what you actually do now. Tell me about the last time..."

**If you're getting wish lists:**
"That's interesting - why is that feature important? What problem would it solve for you?"
