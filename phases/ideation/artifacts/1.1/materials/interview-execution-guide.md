## Overview

### Venture Context

**Venture**: Karachi Grocery App
**Problem Space**: Grocery shopping experience in Karachi, Pakistan
**Initial Hypothesis**: Residents of Karachi face challenges with grocery shopping that could be addressed through a digital solution

### Interview Goals

This interview guide is designed to validate:
1. **Problem Existence**: Is there a real, painful problem worth solving?
2. **Problem Severity**: How much do people currently struggle with this?
3. **Current Workarounds**: What are people doing today to cope?
4. **Solution Resonance**: Does our approach align with what customers actually need?
5. **Willingness to Pay**: Would customers pay for a solution, and how much?

### Success Criteria

To achieve a **GO** decision for Problem Validation (Recipe 1.1), you need:
- **10-15 completed interviews** across target personas
- **Pain Intensity Score ‚â•7/10** (average across interviews)
- **‚â•60% active workarounds** (showing severity)
- **Recent problem incidence** (<7 days for ‚â•70% of interviewees)
- **Solution Interest Score ‚â•7/10**
- **‚â•50% would displace** current solution
- **Willingness to Pay** demonstrated with budget confirmation

## Target Personas

Interview a diverse mix of personas to capture different use cases and pain points:

### Persona 1: Busy Professional Family
- **Segment**: Urban middle-class household
- **Profile**:
  - Age: 28-45 years old
  - Occupation: Working professionals (both spouses typically employed)
  - Household: 3-6 family members
  - Location: DHA, Clifton, Gulshan, North Nazimabad
  - Income: PKR 150,000 - 400,000/month household income
- **Why Interview**: Primary decision-makers and purchasers, highest willingness to pay, time-constrained
- **Interview Focus**:
  - Time constraints and convenience needs
  - Quality expectations and trust factors
  - Price sensitivity vs convenience trade-off
  - Current shopping frequency and basket size
- **Recommended Count**: 5-6 interviews (40% of total)

### Persona 2: Stay-at-Home Parent/Homemaker
- **Segment**: Traditional household manager
- **Profile**:
  - Age: 25-50 years old
  - Occupation: Manages household full-time
  - Household: 4-8 family members (often joint family)
  - Location: All areas (Gulshan, FB Area, PECHS, Malir, North Karachi)
  - Income: PKR 80,000 - 250,000/month household income
- **Why Interview**: Frequent shoppers, price-conscious, quality-focused, understand market well
- **Interview Focus**:
  - Daily/weekly shopping routines and pain points
  - Quality assessment methods (freshness, authenticity)
  - Relationship with local vendors
  - Price comparison behaviors
- **Recommended Count**: 3-4 interviews (25-30% of total)

### Persona 3: Young Single Professional/Student
- **Segment**: Individual living alone or with roommates
- **Profile**:
  - Age: 22-35 years old
  - Occupation: Young professionals, graduate students, newly independent
  - Household: Living alone or with 1-3 roommates
  - Location: Near universities/offices (Clifton, Saddar, Gulshan, North Nazimabad)
  - Income: PKR 40,000 - 150,000/month personal income
- **Why Interview**: Tech-savvy, convenience-driven, early adopters, different shopping patterns
- **Interview Focus**:
  - Minimal shopping frequency but high inconvenience
  - Digital payment preferences
  - Impulse vs planned shopping
  - Delivery time expectations
- **Recommended Count**: 2-3 interviews (20% of total)

### Persona 4: Small Business Owner (Retail/Restaurant)
- **Segment**: B2B potential customer
- **Profile**:
  - Business: Small restaurant, cafe, or retail shop
  - Size: 2-10 employees
  - Location: Commercial areas across Karachi
  - Monthly grocery spend: PKR 100,000 - 500,000
- **Why Interview**: Bulk purchasing needs, different pain points, higher transaction value
- **Interview Focus**:
  - Sourcing challenges and supplier reliability
  - Credit/payment terms needs
  - Delivery timing and frequency requirements
  - Quality consistency needs
- **Recommended Count**: 1-2 interviews (10-15% of total)

**Total Target**: 10-15 interviews distributed as above

## Interview Script

### Pre-Interview Setup (5 minutes before call)

**Environment**:
- Quiet space with good internet/phone connection
- Notebook or laptop for notes
- Recording device (with permission)
- Printed copy of this script

**Mindset**:
- You are a LEARNER, not a seller
- Their experience is the truth, not your assumptions
- Silence is golden - let them think and elaborate
- Follow interesting threads, don't rigidly stick to script

### Introduction (2-3 minutes)

**Script**:

> "Hi [Name], thanks so much for taking the time to chat with me today. This should take about 30 minutes.
>
> Just to set context - I'm researching grocery shopping experiences in Karachi. I'm not here to pitch you anything or sell you on an idea. I genuinely want to understand how you shop today, what works, and what frustrates you.
>
> There are no right or wrong answers - I'm just looking for your honest experience.
>
> **Can I record this conversation just for my notes?** I won't share it with anyone, just want to make sure I capture everything accurately. [Wait for consent]
>
> Before we start, do you have any questions for me?"

**If they ask "What's the product?":**
> "I'm still in the research phase trying to understand if there's even a real problem worth solving. I don't have a product yet - that's why your input is so valuable. I promise I'll share what I'm thinking later in the call, but I want to hear your unbiased experience first."

## Section 1: Problem Discovery (20 minutes)

**Goal**: Understand current behavior, pain points, frequency, and severity WITHOUT mentioning your solution.

---

### Q1: Shopping Routine Discovery

**Question**: "Tell me about the last time you went grocery shopping. Walk me through exactly what happened from start to finish."

**Purpose**:
- Understand actual behavior (not idealized version)
- Identify pain points organically
- Map the current shopping journey
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.1 (Problem Recency), I1.2 (Pain Intensity)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What day was that? What time did you go?"
- "How did you decide what to buy? Did you make a list?"
- "How did you get to the store? How long did that take?"
- "What was the experience like in the store?"
- "How long were you there? What took the most time?"
- "Did anything frustrate you or slow you down?"
- "How did you get everything home?"
- "How much did you spend roughly?"

**Red Flags to Listen For**:
- üö© Can't remember specific details (suggests infrequent activity)
- üö© Describes idealized process, not real experience
- üö© No frustrations mentioned (either lying or no real pain)
- ‚úÖ Rich detail = genuine, recent experience

---

### Q2: Frequency & Patterns

**Question**: "How often do you typically buy groceries? Has this changed over time?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand market segment behavior
- Identify frequency-related pain points
- Assess market size potential
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.1 (Problem Recency)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Do you do one big shop or multiple small trips?"
- "Has your frequency changed in the last year? Why?"
- "Are there certain items you buy more frequently than others?"
- "Do you shop differently for different types of items?" (e.g., fresh vs packaged)

**Red Flags**:
- üö© Very infrequent shopping (<1x/month) may indicate low engagement
- ‚úÖ Regular patterns = predictable need

---

### Q3: Pain Point Exploration

**Question**: "What's the most frustrating thing about buying groceries right now?"

**Purpose**:
- Directly identify top-of-mind pain points
- Measure emotional intensity
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.2 (Pain Intensity), I1.4 (Workaround Activity)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Can you give me a specific example of when this happened recently?"
- "How does this affect you or your family?"
- "How often does this happen?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how much does this frustrate you? Why that number?"
- "Have you complained about this to family or friends?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© Vague, generic complaints ("it's just inconvenient")
- üö© Low intensity frustration (<5/10)
- ‚úÖ Specific, emotional, recent = real pain

---

### Q4: Alternative Exploration

**Question**: "Walk me through all the different ways you currently get groceries. Have you tried different approaches?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand competitive landscape
- Identify what they've tried and why they stuck with current method
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.4 (Workaround Activity), I1.8 (Competitive Displacement)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Have you tried [online grocery services, specific stores, kiryana delivery]?"
- "What did you like or not like about those?"
- "Why did you stop using them or keep using them?"
- "Are you using any of these services right now? How often?"
- "What would need to be different for you to switch?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© Never tried alternatives = low pain or satisfied with status quo
- ‚úÖ Tried multiple solutions = active problem-solving = high pain

---

### Q5: Workaround Investigation

**Question**: "Have you found any tricks or hacks to make grocery shopping easier? What do you do to deal with [pain point they mentioned]?"

**Purpose**:
- Identify active workarounds (signal of real pain)
- Understand current coping mechanisms
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.4 (Workaround Activity)

**Probing Questions**:
- "When did you start doing that?"
- "How much time/money does that save you?"
- "Does it fully solve the problem or just make it better?"
- "Would you rather not have to do that?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© No workarounds = either no real pain OR unsolvable problem
- ‚úÖ Active workarounds = real pain + belief it's solvable

---

### Q6: Impact Assessment

**Question**: "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about grocery shopping, what would it be? Why that specifically?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand priority pain points
- Assess willingness to change
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.2 (Pain Intensity)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What would be different in your life if that was fixed?"
- "How much time would that save you per week?"
- "What would you do with that time?"
- "How much would that be worth to you?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© Asks for feature they can't articulate benefit for
- ‚úÖ Clear, measurable impact = valuable problem

---

### Q7: Comparison to Past

**Question**: "How has your grocery shopping experience changed over the past 5 years? What's better, what's worse?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand market evolution
- Identify deteriorating vs improving aspects
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.1 (Problem Recency)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What changed specifically?"
- "Why do you think it got worse/better?"
- "Are there things you used to do that you can't do anymore?"

---

### Q8: Sharing Behavior

**Question**: "Have you ever vented to friends or family about grocery shopping? What did you say? What did they say?"

**Purpose**:
- Validate pain is shared/common
- Check if problem is socially discussed
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.3 (Active Search Behavior)

**Probing Questions**:
- "When was the last time you had that conversation?"
- "Did they share similar frustrations?"
- "Have you ever searched online for solutions or alternatives?"
- "What did you search for? Did you find anything useful?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© No discussion = isolated problem or low pain
- ‚úÖ Frequent venting = validated pain

## Section 2: Solution Exploration (20 minutes)

**Goal**: Present solution concept NEUTRALLY, assess genuine interest vs politeness, measure alignment with desired outcomes.

**‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Mindset Shift**: You are NOT pitching. You are describing something that might exist and checking if it actually solves their problem. Listen for genuine excitement vs polite interest.

---

### Q9: Ideal Solution Description

**Question**: "Before I tell you what I'm thinking, how would you ideally want this [specific pain point they mentioned] to be solved?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand their mental model of a solution
- Check if your solution aligns with their expectations
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.7 (Desired Outcome Alignment)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What would the experience look like?"
- "How would you want it to work?"
- "What's non-negotiable for you in a solution?"

---

### Q10: Solution Concept Introduction

**Question/Statement**:
> "OK, here's what I'm exploring - and I want your honest reaction, not polite interest. Please tell me if this doesn't actually solve your problem.
>
> [INSERT YOUR 2-SENTENCE SOLUTION CONCEPT HERE]
>
> What's your gut reaction to that?"

**Example for Karachi Grocery App**:
> "I'm exploring a grocery delivery service specifically for Karachi that focuses on [X key benefit]. Unlike existing services, it would [Y differentiation].
>
> What's your gut reaction to that?"

**Purpose**:
- Measure genuine vs polite interest
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.5 (Solution Interest Score)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What specifically do you like or not like?"
- "Is that actually solving the problem you described earlier?"
- "What's missing from that description?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© "That sounds interesting" (polite, not excited)
- üö© "I'd have to see it" (non-committal)
- üö© Long pause before positive response (thinking of politeness)
- ‚úÖ "Oh wait, so you mean..." (engaged, clarifying)
- ‚úÖ Immediate specific questions about how it works
- ‚úÖ "That would actually help with [specific pain]"

---

### Q11: Problem-Solution Fit Check

**Question**: "On a scale of 1-10, how well would this solve the specific frustrations you told me about earlier?"

**Purpose**:
- Quantify solution-problem alignment
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.5 (Solution Interest Score)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Why that score and not higher/lower?"
- "What would make it a 10?"
- "What am I missing?"
- "Does this solve the right problem?"

---

### Q12: Behavior Change Probe

**Question**: "If this existed today, would you use it? Walk me through specifically how you'd use it."

**Purpose**:
- Check if they can visualize using it
- Identify adoption barriers
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.5 (Solution Interest Score), I1.8 (Competitive Displacement)

**Probing Questions**:
- "When would you first try it?"
- "How often would you use it?"
- "What would trigger you to remember to use it?"
- "What would stop you from using it?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© "Yeah, I'd probably try it" (non-specific)
- üö© Can't describe usage scenario
- ‚úÖ Detailed usage description = genuine interest

---

### Q13: Displacement Assessment

**Question**: "If you started using this, what would you stop doing? Would this replace [current solution they mentioned], or use it alongside?"

**Purpose**:
- Measure competitive displacement potential
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.8 (Competitive Displacement)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What percentage of your grocery shopping would shift to this?"
- "What would you still do the old way? Why?"
- "What would have to be true for you to fully switch?"

---

### Q14: Feature Priority Check

**Question**: "If you could only have 3 features from this solution, which 3 would be most important to you?"

**Purpose**:
- Identify MVP requirements
- Separate nice-to-haves from must-haves
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.7 (Desired Outcome Alignment)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Why those specifically?"
- "Would you use it without [feature they didn't pick]?"
- "What's the minimum you'd need for this to be useful?"

---

### Q15: Trust & Risk Exploration

**Question**: "What concerns would you have about using something like this? What would make you hesitant?"

**Purpose**:
- Identify adoption barriers
- Uncover hidden objections
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.5 (Solution Interest Score)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What would need to be true for you to trust this?"
- "Have you had bad experiences with [similar services]?"
- "What would make you quit using it?"

---

### Q16: Outcome Visualization

**Question**: "If you used this successfully for 3 months, what would be different in your life? How would you know it's working?"

**Purpose**:
- Check if solution delivers meaningful outcome
- Validate desired outcome alignment
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.7 (Desired Outcome Alignment)

**Probing Questions**:
- "How would you measure success?"
- "What would you be doing more/less of?"
- "How would your family notice?"

## Section 3: Willingness to Pay (20 minutes)

**Goal**: Understand budget reality, current spend, and pricing expectations WITHOUT anchoring.

**‚ö†Ô∏è Most Important Section**: This is where politeness goes to die. Many people will say they love your idea but won't pay. Be direct.

---

### Q17: Current Spend Baseline

**Question**: "Roughly how much do you spend on groceries per month? Just a ballpark."

**Purpose**:
- Establish baseline budget
- Understand market size
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.6 (Budget Availability)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Is that just groceries or also household items?"
- "How many people are you feeding?"
- "Has that amount changed over time?"
- "Do you feel that's high, low, or about right?"

---

### Q18: Additional Spend Exploration

**Question**: "Do you currently spend any money specifically to make grocery shopping more convenient? Like delivery fees, premium stores, or hiring help?"

**Purpose**:
- Identify existing WTP for convenience
- Understand current "premium" spend
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.6 (Budget Availability), I1.9 (Purchase Intent)

**Probing Questions**:
- "How much do you spend on that per month?"
- "Do you feel you're getting value for that?"
- "Would you spend more if the solution was better?"

---

### Q19: Budget Reallocation Check

**Question**: "If something could save you [X time/Y frustration] with grocery shopping, is there room in your budget to pay for that?"

**Purpose**:
- Check budget availability
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.6 (Budget Availability)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Where would that money come from? Would you cut something else?"
- "Do you make that kind of spending decision yourself or need to discuss?"
- "How do you typically decide if a service is worth paying for?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© "I'd have to talk to my husband/wife" (not the buyer)
- üö© "Money is tight right now" (budget-constrained)
- üö© Long pause before answering

---

### Q20: Value Perception

**Question**: "What's more valuable to you: saving time, saving money, or improving quality? If you had to pick one."

**Purpose**:
- Understand value drivers
- Inform pricing strategy
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.7 (Desired Outcome Alignment)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Why that one specifically?"
- "How much of [time/money/quality] would make this worth it?"

### Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Analysis

**Introduction**:
> "I'm going to ask you 4 quick questions about pricing. There's no right answer - just your gut feeling based on a monthly subscription for this service."

---

### Q21: Too Cheap (Quality Floor)

**Question**: "At what price per month would you think this service is so inexpensive that you'd question its quality or reliability?"

**Purpose**:
- Establish quality floor
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.10 (Price Sensitivity via Van Westendorp)

**Format**: PKR amount per month

---

### Q22: Good Value (Optimal Price Point - Low)

**Question**: "At what price per month would you consider this service to be a good value for the money?"

**Purpose**:
- Establish lower bound of optimal price
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.10 (Price Sensitivity via Van Westendorp)

**Format**: PKR amount per month

---

### Q23: Getting Expensive (Optimal Price Point - High)

**Question**: "At what price per month would you think this service is getting expensive, but you'd still consider it?"

**Purpose**:
- Establish upper bound of optimal price
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.10 (Price Sensitivity via Van Westendorp)

**Format**: PKR amount per month

---

### Q24: Too Expensive (Price Ceiling)

**Question**: "At what price per month would this service be so expensive that you would never consider buying it?"

**Purpose**:
- Establish price ceiling
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.10 (Price Sensitivity via Van Westendorp)

**Format**: PKR amount per month

**Note**: Capture exact numbers. Van Westendorp analysis requires plotting these 4 data points across all interviews.

---

### Q25: Pricing Rationale

**Question**: "Walk me through how you came up with those numbers. What were you comparing it to in your mind?"

**Purpose**:
- Understand pricing anchors
- Validate reference points

**Probing Questions**:
- "Are you thinking of this like [Netflix/delivery fees/something else]?"
- "What other services do you pay monthly for?"
- "How do you decide if a subscription is worth keeping?"

---

### Q26: Purchase Intent Assessment

**Question**: "OK, here's the real test: If I told you this service costs [PRICE IN THEIR 'GOOD VALUE' RANGE] per month and launches next week in [their area], would you sign up?"

**Purpose**:
- Measure genuine purchase intent
- Separate interest from commitment
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.9 (Purchase Intent)

**Probing Questions**:
- "What would make you actually pull out your credit card and sign up?"
- "What would you need to see before committing?"
- "If your answer is 'maybe,' what would turn it into a 'yes'?"

**Red Flags**:
- üö© "Probably" or "I think so" = NO
- üö© "I'd have to see it first" = NO
- üö© "Send me a link" = Polite NO
- ‚úÖ "Yes, where do I sign up?" = YES
- ‚úÖ "Can you put me on a waitlist?" = YES

**Scoring Guide**:
- **5/5** (Strong Yes): "Yes, I'd sign up today" + can articulate why
- **4/5** (Likely Yes): "Yes, assuming [1 reasonable condition]"
- **3/5** (Tentative): "Maybe, I'd have to [see/try/think about it]"
- **2/5** (Unlikely): "Probably not, but..."
- **1/5** (No): "No" or polite deflection

---

### Q27: Referral Willingness

**Question**: "If you used this and it worked well, would you tell your friends/family about it? Why or why not?"

**Purpose**:
- Assess viral potential
- Validate problem universality
- **Feeds KPI**: I1.9 (Purchase Intent - as proxy for enthusiasm)

**Probing Questions**:
- "Who specifically would you tell?"
- "What would you say to them?"
- "Do you think they have the same frustrations?"

## The Mom Test Reminders

### During the Interview

‚úÖ **DO**:
- Ask about specific past experiences: "Tell me about the last time..."
- Listen for pain in their voice and words
- Dig into the details: "How long did that take?" "How much did you spend?"
- Ask about current behavior: "What do you do today when..."
- Stay quiet and let them think
- Follow interesting threads even if not on script

‚ùå **DON'T**:
- Ask hypothetical questions: "Would you use...?"
- Pitch your solution before understanding their problem
- Lead the witness: "Don't you think X is frustrating?"
- Talk more than they do
- Jump to the next question when there's silence
- Defend your idea when they criticize it

### Red Flags You're Breaking The Mom Test

üö© They're complimenting your idea a lot ("That's brilliant!")
üö© You're talking more than they are
üö© They're giving vague answers ("Yeah, that's annoying")
üö© You're defending your solution when they raise concerns
üö© You haven't asked about their last actual experience

### Green Flags You're Doing It Right

‚úÖ They're sharing specific stories with details
‚úÖ They're venting about frustrations without prompting
‚úÖ They're asking YOU questions about how it would work
‚úÖ They're pushing back on your solution with real concerns

### Field Definitions

**Pain Score (0-10)**: How intensely they feel the problem (ask directly + infer from emotion)
- 9-10: Extreme pain, unprompted venting, emotional
- 7-8: Clear frustration, specific examples, measurable impact
- 5-6: Moderate annoyance, but not urgent
- 3-4: Mild inconvenience, hard to articulate
- 0-2: No real pain, hypothetical problem

**Workarounds**: Do they actively do something to cope with the problem?
- Y = Yes (specific workaround they do regularly)
- N = No (just accept the pain)

**Problem Recency**: When did they last experience this problem?
- <7d = Within past week (recent, active problem)
- <30d = Within past month (regular problem)
- >30d = More than a month ago (infrequent or fading memory)

**Solution Interest (0-10)**: How interested are they in YOUR solution concept?
- 9-10: "Where do I sign up?", asks detailed questions, genuine excitement
- 7-8: "That would really help", can articulate specific benefits
- 5-6: "Sounds interesting", polite interest
- 3-4: "Maybe", non-committal
- 0-2: "Not for me" or clearly disinterested

**Displacement %**: What % of current solution would they replace?
- 100% = Full replacement
- 50-99% = Primary solution
- 20-49% = Supplementary solution
- <20% = Occasional use only

**Outcome Align**: Does solution deliver outcome they care about?
- Y = Solution clearly maps to desired outcome
- N = Solution doesn't match what they want to achieve

**Budget Available**: Do they have money to pay for this?
- Y = Clear budget, already spending on alternatives or can reallocate
- N = No budget, money is tight, would have to cut something else essential

**Van Westendorp Pricing**: Record exact PKR amounts for all 4 price points

**Purchase Intent (1-5)**:
- 5 = "Yes, where do I sign up?"
- 4 = "Yes, if [1 reasonable condition]"
- 3 = "Maybe, I'd need to..."
- 2 = "Probably not"
- 1 = "No" or polite deflection

**Top 3 Pain Points**: List their specific top frustrations (in their words)

**Notes**: Any surprising insights, quotes, referrals, or context

## Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

### Pitfall 1: The Politeness Trap
**Problem**: Interviewees say nice things about your idea to be polite.
**Solution**:
- Ask about past behavior, not future intentions
- Listen for vague vs specific language
- Watch for long pauses before positive responses
- Ask: "What would make you NOT use this?"

### Pitfall 2: Talking Too Much
**Problem**: You pitch instead of listen.
**Solution**:
- Set a timer - if you talk >30% of the time, you're pitching
- Write down your thoughts instead of saying them
- Use silence - wait 5 seconds after they stop talking

### Pitfall 3: Confirmation Bias
**Problem**: Only hearing what confirms your idea.
**Solution**:
- Actively listen for contradictions
- Ask: "Why wouldn't this work?"
- Note surprises and challenges explicitly

### Pitfall 4: Hypothetical Questions
**Problem**: Asking "Would you...?" instead of "When did you...?"
**Solution**:
- Rephrase: "Would you use X?" ‚Üí "Tell me about the last time you needed X"
- Focus on past behavior, not future predictions

### Pitfall 5: Fishing for Features
**Problem**: Asking what features they want.
**Solution**:
- Ask about their current workflow and pain points
- Let them describe the outcome, not the solution
- Features come from patterns, not feature requests
